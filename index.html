Exploring the Mysteries of Dark Energy: Implications for the Accelerating Universe

Admir Bauermeister, Atif Ziko, Semir Agic, Admir Mahmutovic, Omer Mahmutovic, Asim Numanovic, Faruk Kurtic, Alen Mehicic

The enigmatic and elusive form of energy known as dark energy, which pervades the vast expanse of the universe, continues to be one of the most perplexing mysteries in the fields of physical cosmology and astronomy. Initially, the detection of dark energy was made possible through the observation of supernovae, and it is now widely believed that it accounts for a significant proportion of the universe's overall energy content. Despite its crucial significance, the fundamental nature of dark energy remains shrouded in mystery, with many questions about its properties still unanswered. This paper aims to provide an overview of the existing evidence for dark energy, as well as exploring its role in the wider evolution of the universe. As scientists continue to grapple with the complexities of this enigmatic phenomenon, a combination of theoretical and observational approaches is being employed to shed light on its properties. The ongoing efforts to understand the nature of dark energy have far-reaching implications for our understanding of the cosmos as a whole, and it is hoped that continued research will eventually lead to a deeper understanding of this elusive energy form.

Dark energy, a highly perplexing and elusive form of energy that pervades the vast and seemingly infinite expanse of the cosmos, wields a profound and undeniable influence over the largest scales of the universe, posing an enduring and confounding enigma in the realms of physical cosmology and astronomy. The initial indication of its existence emerged from observations of supernovae, which revealed that the expansion of the universe is not linear but, rather, accelerating. This discovery upended earlier theories that postulated all forms of matter and energy would inevitably cause the universe's expansion to decelerate over time, leading scientists to the realization that a new form of energy was required to explain this perplexing phenomenon. While the hot Big Bang model, which provides an explanation for the universe's inception, can be accounted for through general relativity's subsequent large-scale motion, the acceleration of the universe's expansion could only be explained by introducing a new form of energy. Since the 1990s, dark energy has been the most widely accepted explanation for this enigmatic phenomenon. At present, extensive research in cosmology is underway to unravel the fundamental nature of dark energy, with scientists employing a combination of theoretical and observational approaches to shed light on this mysterious and elusive energy form. Assuming the validity of the lambda-CDM model of cosmology, current measurements indicate that dark energy constitutes a staggering 68% of the observable universe's total energy, dwarfing the contributions of dark matter and ordinary matter, which make up 26% and 5%, respectively, with the negligible amounts provided by neutrinos and photons. Although the density of dark energy, which stands at 6×10−10 J/m3 (~7×10−30 g/cm3), is markedly lower than that of ordinary matter or dark matter within galaxies, its uniformity across space makes it the dominant component of the universe's mass-energy composition. Two proposed forms of dark energy include the cosmological constant, which represents a constant energy density that fills space homogeneously, and scalar fields, such as quintessence or moduli, which are dynamic quantities with energy densities that vary in both time and space. It is worth noting that contributions from scalar fields that are constant in space are typically included in the cosmological constant. The cosmological constant can be formulated to be equivalent to the vacuum energy, also known as the zero-point radiation of space. However, scalar fields that change in space can be difficult to distinguish from a cosmological constant, as the changes may be prolonged.

Einstein's Cosmological Constant

Einstein's Cosmological Constant, a term that can be incorporated into the Einstein Field Equations of general relativity, has significant implications for our understanding of the universe. The constant, also referred to as vacuum energy, is conceptually equivalent to the mass of empty space and has been proposed as a means of balancing gravity to create a static universe. Einstein himself initially proposed the constant to obtain a solution to the gravitational field equation that would lead to a stable universe. However, it was later realized that such a universe would not be sustainable, as local inhomogeneities would ultimately lead to the expansion or contraction of the universe. The equilibrium is unstable, as the expansion of the universe would release vacuum energy, causing yet more expansion. Similarly, a contracting universe would continue to contract. Einstein's suggestion that empty space possesses energy, which would not be diluted as space expands, was a groundbreaking idea. As space continues to come into existence, more energy-of-space would be created, leading to accelerated expansion. Nevertheless, the uneven distribution of matter throughout the universe ensures that disturbances like those mentioned are inevitable. Furthermore, Edwin Hubble's observations in 1929 suggested that the universe was expanding and not static. Einstein later referred to his failure to predict the concept of a dynamic universe, as opposed to a static one, as his most significant mistake. In conclusion, the cosmological constant has played a critical role in shaping our understanding of the universe, with its implications extending far beyond Einstein's initial proposals.

Inflationary dark energy represents a theoretical construct in modern cosmology that seeks to reconcile the observed expansion rate of the universe with the predictions of the Big Bang model.

In 1980, two brilliant scientists, Alan Guth and Alexei Starobinsky, proposed a negative pressure field that could have sparked cosmic inflation in the early universe, a repulsive force that is eerily similar to dark energy. This theory suggests that just after the Big Bang, the universe underwent a rapid and exponential expansion, fueled by some unknown, mysterious force. While it is true that inflation must have occurred at a much higher energy density than the dark energy we observe today, it is still unclear if there is any relationship between inflation and dark energy. For a long time, most cosmological research focused on models with critical density in matter only, but in the late 1980s, these models began to face challenges from discrepancies between predicted and observed values for the Hubble constant and large-scale galaxy clustering. These difficulties led to the development of modified CDM models, including the Lambda-CDM model, which became the leading model after direct evidence for dark energy was observed in 1998 through supernova observations. In the established framework of cosmology, the universe is characterized by three fundamental constituents: matter, radiation, and dark energy. Matter is defined as any physical substance whose energy density is inversely proportional to the cube of the scale factor, while radiation encompasses any entity whose energy density scales with the inverse fourth power of the scale factor (ρ ∝ a−4). This phenomenon can be comprehended through a simple conceptualization: if an ordinary particle is enclosed in a cube-shaped container, a doubling of the box edge length would lead to a reduction in density (and thus energy density) by a factor of eight. However, in the case of radiation, spatial distance expansion would result in a larger reduction in energy density due to the redshift effect.

The discovery of the accelerating expansion of the universe through observations of Type Ia supernovae, first reported in 1998 by the High-Z Supernova Search Team and later supported by the Supernova Cosmology Project, represents a seminal moment in modern cosmology. The 2011 Nobel Prize in Physics was awarded to Saul Perlmutter, Brian P. Schmidt, and Adam G. Riess in recognition of their significant contributions to the discovery.

The veracity of this discovery has since been corroborated by numerous independent observations, including those of the cosmic microwave background, gravitational lensing, and large-scale structure of the cosmos, along with refined measurements of supernovae. Some have argued that the sole evidence for dark energy is confined to distance measurements and their associated redshifts, with the cosmic microwave background anisotropies and baryon acoustic oscillations serving only to demonstrate that distances to a given redshift exceed those that would be expected in a "dusty" Friedmann-Lemaître universe, as well as the local Hubble constant.

Type Ia supernovae have proven especially valuable to cosmology due to their status as standard candles of exceptional reliability across vast cosmological distances, enabling researchers to gauge the expansion history of the universe by examining the relationship between redshift and the distance to an object. While the former can be readily measured, determining the latter presents a greater challenge. Astronomers commonly resort to standard candles, for which the intrinsic brightness or absolute magnitude is known, allowing for the measurement of the object's distance from its apparent magnitude. Owing to their remarkable and consistent luminosity, Type Ia supernovae have emerged as the preeminent standard candles across cosmological distances. Current observations of supernovae are consistent with a universe composed of 71.3% dark energy, in addition to 27.4% dark matter and baryonic matter in combination.

The relic radiation of the Big Bang

The existence of dark energy is a crucial element in reconciling the measured geometry of space with the total amount of matter in the universe. Cosmic microwave background anisotropies measurements suggest a nearly flat universe, implying that the mass-energy density of the universe must equal the critical density. However, the total amount of matter, including baryonic and dark matter, as measured from the cosmic microwave background spectrum, accounts for merely 30% of the critical density. This deficit indicates the existence of an additional form of energy, or dark energy, which accounts for the remaining 70% of the universe's composition.

According to the Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe (WMAP) spacecraft's seven-year analysis, the universe is made up of 72.8% dark energy, 22.7% dark matter, and 4.5% ordinary matter. In 2013, further investigations using the Planck spacecraft observations of the cosmic microwave background provided a more precise estimate, indicating that the universe is composed of 68.3% dark energy, 26.8% dark matter, and 4.9% ordinary matter.

Empirical measurements of the Hubble constant

In recent years, the observational Hubble constant data (OHD), also known as cosmic chronometers, has been gaining significant attention as a new approach to test evidence of dark energy. The Hubble constant, H(z), is measured as a function of cosmological redshift, and OHD directly tracks the expansion history of the universe through passively evolving early-type galaxies, which serve as "cosmic chronometers." This approach provides standard clocks in the universe and measures the differential age evolution as a function of redshift of these cosmic chronometers to provide a direct estimate of the Hubble parameter. The differential quantity, Δz/Δt, brings more information and is appealing for computation, as it can minimize many common issues and systematic effects. This method has been widely used to examine the accelerated cosmic expansion and study properties of dark energy due to its ability to measure the Hubble parameter directly, unlike analyses of supernovae and baryon acoustic oscillations, which are based on integrals of the Hubble parameter.

Λ - Cosmological Constant

The concept of dark energy can be attributed to an inherent and elemental energy of the universe, which is typically referred to as the cosmological constant represented by the Greek letter Λ, also known as the Lambda-CDM model. According to Einstein's theory of general relativity, since mass and energy are linked by the equation E=mc², it is expected that this energy will have a gravitational effect. This energy density of space, also known as vacuum energy, exists within the void and contributes to the universe's expansion. However, a critical issue with this theory is that it predicts a massive cosmological constant that is nearly 120 orders of magnitude larger than the observed value. It is proposed that this mismatch can be reconciled by a delicate cancellation between a large positive term and an equally large but negative term. Furthermore, specific supersymmetric theories dictate a cosmological constant that is precisely zero, while the existence of a metastable vacuum state with a positive cosmological constant remains ambiguous in string theory. Nonetheless, the inability to identify such a state does not negate the possibility of other dark energy models such as quintessence, which can be compatible with string theory.

Quintessence

In models of dark energy known as quintessence, the acceleration of the scale factor is attributed to the potential energy of a dynamical field referred to as the quintessence field. In contrast to the cosmological constant, the quintessence field can vary both in space and time. To avoid the formation of structure similar to matter, the quintessence field must be exceedingly light with a large Compton wavelength. The simplest scenarios for the quintessence field involve a canonical kinetic term, minimal coupling to gravity, and no higher order operations in its Lagrangian. While no concrete evidence for quintessence exists, it also remains unrefuted. In general, quintessence predicts a slightly slower acceleration of the universe's expansion than the cosmological constant. Some researchers suggest that evidence for quintessence may be found in violations of Einstein's equivalence principle or the variation of fundamental constants in space or time. Scalar fields, predicted by both the Standard Model of particle physics and string theory, face similar issues as the cosmological constant problem or the problem of constructing models of cosmological inflation, with renormalization theory predicting that scalar fields should acquire large masses. The coincidence problem, which questions why the universe's acceleration began when it did, may be addressed by the "tracker" behavior of many quintessence models. In these models, the quintessence field's density closely tracks, but is less than, the radiation density until matter-radiation equality, triggering the quintessence field to start behaving as dark energy and eventually dominate the universe. This naturally sets the low energy scale of the dark energy. In 2004, scientists found that the equation of state of dark energy may have crossed the cosmological constant boundary from above to below. However, a no-go theorem has since been proved, suggesting that such a scenario requires models with at least two types of quintessence, resulting in the so-called Quintom scenario. Some special cases of quintessence include phantom energy, where the energy density of quintessence increases with time, and k-essence, which has a non-standard form of kinetic energy such as negative kinetic energy. These can have unusual properties, with phantom energy, for instance, potentially causing a Big Rip. Recently, a group of researchers argued that observations of the Hubble tension may imply that only quintessence models with a nonzero coupling constant are viable.

Interacting dark energy: Unified Dark Theory, Gravitational Modification Theory, Dark Matter-Energy Synthesis

One category of theories that seeks to provide a comprehensive explanation for both dark matter and dark energy is the class of interacting dark energy theories, which aim to unify these phenomena into a single entity that modifies the laws of gravity at various scales. These theories propose various possibilities, such as treating dark energy and dark matter as different manifestations of the same mysterious substance or suggesting that cold dark matter decays into dark energy. Some of these theories propose covariant theories of modified gravities, which alter the dynamics of spacetime to account for the effects attributed to dark energy and dark matter. While it is possible for dark energy to interact with both the rest of the dark sector and ordinary matter, the strength of the coupling between dark energy and baryons is difficult to constrain using cosmology alone, requiring other indirect techniques or laboratory searches. Recent research has suggested that a chameleon model of dark energy may be responsible for the unexplained excess observed in the XENON1T detector in Italy. On the other hand, variable dark energy models propose that the density of dark energy has varied over time during the history of the universe. Using modern observational data, such models can be constrained and tested, with the Chevallier-Polarski-Linder model being one of the most widely studied, along with other models proposed by Barboza and Alcaniz, Jassal et al., Wetterich, and Oztas et al.
